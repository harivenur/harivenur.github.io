Wildlife Conservation
Central & South America, Africa, Southeast Asia

During my time with the Fauna & Flora International (FFI) in Washington D.C., I wrote many successful grant proposals and project reports for a wide range of wildlife conservation projects, all supported by The United States Fish and Wildlife Service. The projects aimed at conservation of endangered species and landscapes, and empowerment of local stakeholders, throughout the tropical regions of Africa (Cross-River gorilla in Nigeria, chimps in Uganda and Liberia, forest elephants in Guinea), Asia (Siam crocodiles and Asian elephants in Cambodia, Sumatran Tiger and Kloss's Gibbon in Indonesia, Hainan Gibbon in China, and Tonkin snub-nosed monkey in Vietnam), and Latin America and the Caribbean (Turtles in Nicaragua, the Antiguan Racer in Antigua, Maya Golden Landscape and Bladen Nature Reserve in Belize, Awacachi Corridor in Ecuador, Ometepe island in Nicaragua, and stakeholder-engagement in Brazilian Amazon). Though I was originally hired as a part of an idea to initiate an American wing of the the Global Trees Campaign jointly run by FFI and Botanical Garden Conservation International, the project did not receive an official greenflag during my months at FFI.

Indian subcontinent

My work for the Wildlife Trust of India was based from their headquarters in Delhi NCR. For almost a year, I worked to develop wildlife conservation project plans for field teams in ~16 states in India. Then I moved to grant proposal writing, report writing, fundraising, dealing with donors, and building databases. In both roles, I worked on a wide variety of conservation projects, mostly focussed on large mammals (tiger, elephant, rhino, gibbons, etc.), birds (Sarus crane), ungulates (markhor and wild water buffalo), reptiles (gharial), marine fishes (whale shark). It also included ecosystem and landscape conservation projects (mangroves in Kerala, Manas in Assam, elephant corridors throughout the subcontinent) and assisting the forest departments in wildlife management (through running rescue centers, providing insurance to forest personnel, giving training and equipment to guards, assisting in human-wildlife conflicts, poaching, wildlife crime, and wildlife-related legal cases, reducing railroad accidents involving large mammals, etc.). Working on the fine details of these projects, interacting with field staff, and travelling to conservation spaces, I gained both a broad and a nuanced knowledge of India's forests and conservation conditions.
