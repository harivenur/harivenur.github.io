Ecology
Tree-fungal interactions in moist tropical forests of Panama

Postdoctoral Research

Have you even thought about the death of those huge trees in rainforests, and about what happens when they die? Most big trees die when their stems break when they become brittle by getting internally eaten by sly creatures such as fungi or when they get hit by lightning, wind, etc., or by a combination of such forces. They profoundly change the forest with their death. They create a huge gap in the forest where they stood, and also kill and maim many other trees and other plants around them. I am interested in how a tree get weakened and killed internally by fungi. Internal wood decay (or heart rot) cannot be seen until the tree breaks. In the famed Barro Colorado Island in Panama, working with Dr. Erin Spear (Smithsonian Tropical Research Institute), Dr. Evan Gora (Cary Institute) and Dr. Greg Gilbert (UC Santa Cruz), I take cross-sectional scan of trees using sound waves (sonic tomography!). The idea is to find the rot, and see if it is linked to any historic damage, growth and mortality, and ultimately, with forest dynamics and ecosystem functioning.

Herb diversity in dry tropical forests of India

Doctoral Research

Sometimes we think we know quite a lot about plants in tropical forests. In reality, what we think we know is mostly about the large, woody ones, you know, those heavy guys called trees. What do we know about the smaller ones, those herbaceous plants down there on the ground? They don't seem relevant? If not, you have fallen for the tree-propaganda (timber, carbon, biomass, and all!). Those small herbaceous ones have been long ignored by tropical ecologists. If you think about it, most of world's food comes from those tropical herbs (remember rice and maize? then potato, yams, millets, sweet potato, cucumber, melons, pumpkin, turmeric, ginger, ... I hope you got the idea). Billions of people depend on tropical herbs for food, medicine, and what not! For my PhD work, I thought of looking into herb diversity variation in dry tropical forests and what I found was crazy! Read about it in my upcoming publications.

Heteropsis and its soils in rainforests of Venezuela

Master's Research

In the rainforests of South America lives a wonderful plant genus called Heteropsis. This plant sprouts on the ground and starts climbing trees. Once it finds a cozy place up in the high canopy, it cuts its ground connections, and sends new long aerial roots all the way down to the ground again to suck nutrients up. This behavior makes Heteropsis a 'secondary hemiepiphyte'. The smart indigenous people of the region cut these aerial roots, and use it like Asians use rattan (Calamus, etc.)and reed bamboo (Ochlandra). Living with the hunter-gatherer Ye'kuana people in deep Caura forests of Venezuela, I collected data on two species of this genus: H. flexuosa and H. spruceana, to see if the plant cares about disturbances. I found that this sensitive plant grows only on trees in old-growth forests, while totally avoiding disturbed forests, even after many years of recovery, despite abundant seed supply from the forest around it. My data suggests that probably its seeds fail to germinate in the high-clay topsoil of disturbed forests.
