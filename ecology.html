Ecology
Herb diversity in dry tropical forests of India

Doctoral Research

Sometimes we think we know quite a lot about plants in tropical forests. In reality, what we think we know is mostly about the large, woody ones, you know, those heavy guys called trees. What do we know about the smaller ones, those herbaceous plants down there on the ground? They don't seem relevant? If not, you have fallen for the tree-propaganda (timber, carbon, biomass, and all!). Those small herbaceous ones have been long ignored by tropical ecologists. If you think about it, most of world's food comes from those tropical herbs (remember rice and maize? then potato, yams, millets, sweet potato, cucumber, melons, pumpkin, turmeric, ginger, ... I hope you got the idea). Billions of people depend on tropical herbs for food, medicine, and what not! For my PhD work, I thought of looking into herb diversity variation in dry tropical forests and what I found was crazy! Read about it in my upcoming publications.

To do this work, I got superb guidance from Dr Liza Comita, and quality support from a bunch of scientists, including Dr Simon A. Queenborough at Yale, Dr Raman Sukumar and his lab members at the Centre for Ecological Sciences at the Indian Institute of Science and a long list of botanists in India.

Heteropsis and its soils in rainforests of Venezuela

In the rainforests of South America lives a wonderful plant genus called Heteropsis, who comes from the family Araceae (same as anthuriums and titan arums!). This crazy guy sprouts on the ground and starts climbing trees. Once it finds a cozy place up in the high canopy, it cuts its ground connections, and sends new long aerial roots all the way down to the ground again to suck nutrients up. This behavior makes Heteropsis a 'secondary hemiepiphyte'. The supercool and smart indigenous people of the region cut its really long aerial roots, and use it like Asians use rattan (and a bit more!). Living with the hunter-gatherer Ye'kuana people in deep Caura forests of Venezuela, I collected data on two species of this genus: H. flexuosa and H. spruceana, to see if the plant cares about disturbance by slash-and-burn farming. You know what? I found that this sensitive plant grows only on trees in old-growth forests, and totally avoids disturbed forests, despite abundant seed supply from the forest around it, and high density of trees to climb on. I guess its seeds fail to germinate in the humus-depleted and high-clay topsoil of disturbed forests, even after 40 years of recovery.

For this study, I was guided by Dr Florencia Montagnini, and supported by the Ye'kuana people in the forest-village of Boca de Nichare along the Caura River, and La Asociación Venezolana para la Conservación de Áreas Naturales (ACOANA) in Caracas.
