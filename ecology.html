Doctoral Research
Herb diversity in seasonally dry tropical forests of Western Ghats, India

Sometimes we think we know quite a lot about forests. Ha! Actually we know nothing. What we think we know about plants in those tropical one are because we were addicted to those cool old trees. What do we know about small ones, those wee little plants down there near the ground? You think they are nothing? Think twice. You have just fallen for those tree propaganda. Carbon and tropical rain forest plots and all. Almost half of all plants in dry forests are small ones without wood. When I started thinking about doing a PhD, I didn't hink twice before deciding to work on those small guys, those herbaceous ones, I mean And what I found is crazy! We all thought that as landscapes get wetter and wetter, tree diversity increases, and so must be the diversity of other plants as well. In fact, it goes totally the other way round for herbs. Read more about it in my upcoming publications.

For this work, I got amazing guidance from my advisor, Dr Liza Comita, and abundant help from many taxonomists and botanists in India , especially, the lab of Dr Raman Sukumar at The Centre for Ecological Sciences at the Indian Institute of Science.

Master's Research
Heteropsis and associated soils in Orinoco rainforests, Venezuela

For my Master's Research at Yale, I worked on soils where a secondary hemiepiphyte of the genus Heteropsis (Family: Araceae) in the rainforests of the Caura River, a major tributary of the mighty Orinoco in Venezuela. The plant’s roots are widely used by tribal populations throughout the Guyana-shield rainforests and northern Amazonia as a lashing material, almost like a rattan. Living with the Ye'kuana people in the deep Caura forests, I collected data on two Heteropsis species: H. flexuosa and H. spruceana, to see if the plant is affected by soils disturbed by slash and burn farming. I found that Heteropsis grows only on trees in old-growth forests, and avoids disturbed forests, even after about 40 years of recovery, despite abundant seed dispersal from the old-growth around it. From the soil texture, I guessed this was probably due to the failure of its seeds to germinate in the humus-depleted and high-clay topsoil of disturbed forests.

For this study, I worked under the guidance of Dr Florencia Montagnini, and with the support of the Ye'kuana people of Venezuela and the La Asociación Venezolana para la Conservación de Áreas Naturales (ACOANA) in Caracas, who facilitated the research, especially in getting the nod from Kuyujani, the tribal organization, in making my solo stay possible in the Ye'kuana village of Boca de Nichare, and arranging for the analysis of my samples at the Universidad Central de Venezuela in Caracas. My gratitude towards the amazingly kind Ye'kuana people who hosted and protected me in their forests, is boundless. The results of my study were not published, due to lack of adequate replications, but were shared with ACOANA to aid their work in the Caura forests.
