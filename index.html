About

Hello! I am a tropical ecologist, finishing a PhD at the Yale University in Connecticut, United States. I am professionally trained, skilled and experienced in four fields: tropical forest ecology, wildlife conservation, agriculture and journalism. If you think that's quite a mix, it was achieved over many years of focussed work in a direction that helped me make more sense of the world around me. I have degrees on the first three (from Kerala Agricultural University and Yale), and a diploma in the last one (from Thiruvananthapuram Press Club), and I have worked about a decade with non-governmental agencies and media organizations in the United States and India. I am currently a plant community ecologist, studying diversity in seasonally dry tropical forests (SDTF) for my PhD dissertation. For my dissertation, I am looking at how alpha and beta diversity of terrestrial herbaceous plants (plants without above-ground wood) in the understorey of an SDTF with a strong east-to-west gradient in annual rainfall (775 mm change linearly over 36 km). Herbaceous plants are among the most overlooked group of plants in tropical forests across the world, and I enjoy my work! To learn more about my work, please go ahead and read more on this website.

Ecology: Heteropsis species in Orinoco rainforests

For my Master's Research, I worked on soils where a secondary hemiepiphyte of the genus Heteropsis (Family: Araceae) was found in the Caura River basin of Venezuela's. Caura is a major tributary of the Orinoco River. Working under the guidance of Dr. Florencia Montagnini (Yale), and with the support of the Ye'kuana people of Venezuela and the La Asociación Venezolana para la Conservación de Áreas Naturales (ACOANA) in Caracas, I managed to travel to the deep Caura forests to collect data on Heteropsis species. In one of the most difficult terrains on earth, the Ye'kuana were extremely kind to host me in their village to study the soils preferred by the plant that is widely used in the Guyana-shield rainforests and northern Amazonia as a lashing material, almost like a rattan. I found that Heteropsis grows only on old-growth forests, and avoids disturbed forests, even after about 40 years, despite abundant seed dispersal from the old-growth around it. From the soil texture, I guessed this was probably due to the failure of its seeds to germinate in the depleted topsoil of disturbed forests. The results were not published, given the lack of adequate replications. The results were shared with ACOANA, who helped me in every sense in both Caracas and in Caura, especially in getting the nod from Kuyujani, the tribal organization.
