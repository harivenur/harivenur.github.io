About me

I am a tropical ecologist, finishing a PhD at the Yale University in New Haven, CT, USA. I am professionally trained, skilled and experienced in four fields: tropical forest ecology, wildlife conservation, agriculture, and journalism. If you think that's quite a mix, it was achieved over many years of focussed work in a direction that helped me make more sense of the world around me. I have Bachelor's and Master's degrees on the first three (from Kerala Agricultural University and Yale University), and a diploma in the last one (from Thiruvananthapuram Press Club), and I have worked about a decade with non-governmental conservation agencies, activist groups and professional media houses in the United States and India. I have worked in a range of conservation projects thoughout Central and South America, India, Africa, South East Asia, and Central Asia, mostly on mammals and reptiles, and also on trees. My connection to plants is more special to me than the one to animals or fungi, and I am pretty good with tropical plant families. I have professional skills in English and Malayalam (published in both); I am fluent in Hindi, and speak Spanish and Tamil.

I am currently a plant community ecologist, studying herbaceous plant diversity in seasonally dry tropical forests (SDTF) for my PhD dissertation. Herbaceous plants are cool, and I enjoy my work! After my PhD, I look forward to working more on the ecology of tropical forest plants, especially, their diversity, biotic and abiotic interactions, and how they make sense to the human world.

Explore this simple website to learn about my work and ideas. If you need to know more, if you would like to work with me, or if you have any suggestions, please email me!

Ecology

September 2016 - Present

Doctoral Research
Herb diversity in seasonally dry tropical forests of Western Ghats, India

Much of our understanding of the patterns and drivers on the plant diversity in tropical forests comes from trees, despite trees forming only up to a quarter of all plant species in these forests. It is unclear whether assumptions and predictions based on trees hold good for all other lifeforms, but we continue to do so. Understory herbaceous angiosperms, which could form over 40% of plant biodiversity in some tropical forests, have received relatively little attention. My work tries to uncover the spatial and temporal variation of herb diversity (alpha and beta) in a seasonally dry tropical forest in India's Western Ghats. Rainfall in this protected SDTF, called Mudumalai National Park and Tiger Reserve, changes about 1000 mm from east to west over just 40 km, causing a wide variation in vegetation from scrub forest at one end to semi-evergreen forest at the other. Such a strong rainfall gradient also creates gradients of co-variables, especially, that of understory light. Others have found that diversity and density of trees in this gradient increases with increase in rainfall. How does the diversity and community composition of understory herbs, which could be influenced by both light and water, two critical abiotic factors, and their co-variables, actually fare along such gradients? Look forward to reading my publications.

For my dissertation research, I work under the guidance of Dr Liza Comita, and with abundant help from various taxonomists and botanists in India, especially, the lab of Dr Raman Sukumar at The Centre for Ecological Sciences at the Indian Institute of Science, who hosted me at their Field Research Station in Mudumalai, and supported my work with logistical help and additional data.

September 2008 - March 2010

Master's Research
Heteropsis and associated soils in Orinoco rainforests, Venezuela

For my Master's Research at Yale, I worked on soils where a secondary hemiepiphyte of the genus Heteropsis (Family: Araceae) in the rainforests of the Caura River, a major tributary of the mighty Orinoco in Venezuela. The plant’s roots are widely used by tribal populations throughout the Guyana-shield rainforests and northern Amazonia as a lashing material, almost like a rattan. Living with the Ye'kuana people in the deep Caura forests, I collected data on two Heteropsis species: H. flexuosa and H. spruceana, to see if the plant is affected by soils disturbed by slash and burn farming. I found that Heteropsis grows only on trees in old-growth forests, and avoids disturbed forests, even after about 40 years of recovery, despite abundant seed dispersal from the old-growth around it. From the soil texture, I guessed this was probably due to the failure of its seeds to germinate in the humus-depleted and high-clay topsoil of disturbed forests.

For this study, I worked under the guidance of Dr Florencia Montagnini, and with the support of the Ye'kuana people of Venezuela and the La Asociación Venezolana para la Conservación de Áreas Naturales (ACOANA) in Caracas, who facilitated the research, especially in getting the nod from Kuyujani, the tribal organization, in making my solo stay possible in the Ye'kuana village of Boca de Nichare, and arranging for the analysis of my samples at the Universidad Central de Venezuela in Caracas. My gratitude towards the amazingly kind Ye'kuana people who hosted and protected me in their forests, is boundless. The results of my study were not published, due to lack of adequate replications, but were shared with ACOANA to aid their work in the Caura forests.

I completed my Bachelor's (Science) in Agriculture from the College of Agriculture, Vellayani, Thiruvananthapuram, one of the campuses of the Kerala Agricultural University (KAU). The KAU syllabus had a range of mandatory courses (no electives), ranging from biology courses such as Botany, Plant taxonomy, Entomology, Biochemistry, Analytical Chemistry, and Microbiology to applied courses including Agronomy, Horticulture, Soil Science, Animal Husbandry, and Biotechnology, along with many supportive courses such as Economics, Agricultural Extension, Farm engineering, and Social sciences. 1 Year

Independent Work
Personal Travel, Research and Writing

By the end of my formal (BSc.) studies in Agriculture, I had become disappointed with chemical-based farming, and the collapse of the farming ecosystems in the state of Kerala. Though I wanted to go for a Masters study, some health issues sort of put a temporary hold on my hopes. For almost a year, as and when health allowed, I travelled throughout Kerala and Tamil Nadu, and met with people practicing alternative farming practices such as those influenced by Masanobu Fukuoka and Bill Mollison; farmers trying to save traditional crop varieties, cattle breeds, and farming systems; and activists working on a range of farm-related and environmental subjects. I also met with people working on other agriculture-related sustainable practices such as pottery and bamboo-based products, and on alternative education. With the help of a local birding group, I also learned the basics of bird-watching, mostly in farm ecosystems and protected forests. A main goal of my travels was to learn about alternative farming methods, and understand how Kerala's rice-farming systems collapsed since the 1970's. In the process, I familiarized myself with agricultural and natural landscapes of Kerala and Southern Tamil Nadu, and compiled information on plant-based DIY pesticides, fungicides and bactericides that could be used in agriculture and animal husbandry, and experimented with new combinations of such concoctions with the help of farmers. During the same period, I also started my career as a freelance journalist, and managed to get a couple of my articles on agriculture published. All my travel was supported by my parents during the period.

I formulated the original standards for the 'organic' produce for the market, and helped in evaluation, while another person handled the marketing aspect. Plant Ecology

Herbaceous plant diversity in forest ecosystems: patterns, mechanisms, and threats

Studies conducted in forests have resulted in much of the ecological theory we build upon today. However, our basic understanding of forest ecology comes almost exclusively from the study of trees, even though they represent only a small fraction of the plant diversity present in forests. In recent decades there has been an increasing number of studies of forest herbs, broadening our understanding of plant community ecology in forest ecosystems. Here we highlight ten recent studies examining patterns and drivers of, as well as threats to, herbaceous plant diversity in forests. We first examine local, regional, and global patterns of herbaceous diversity in forests and how such patterns differ for woody versus herbaceous species. We then focus on ecological mechanisms that contribute to forest herb diversity, including the role of abiotic and biotic interactions. We end by discussing some major anthropogenic impacts on forest herb diversity, identifying where herbs are particularly susceptible or particularly resilient to current and predicted changes in comparison to trees.
